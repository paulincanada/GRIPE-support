Frequently asked questions

The short answers, and one long one about taxes.

What is the METC, and why does GRIPE make such a fuss about it?

The Medical Expense Tax Credit is a federal (and provincial) tax credit for medical expenses you paid out of pocket — meaning the portion no insurance plan reimbursed. If your household coordinates two or three plans, that's exactly the number GRIPE already tracks for every receipt, which is why the app is unusually good at this.

The rules, in plain language

Only the unreimbursed part counts. A $120 massage where plan one paid $80 and plan two paid $20 contributes $20 — not $120. Claiming the gross amount is the classic audit magnet; GRIPE nets every expense against what the carriers actually paid.

There's a threshold to beat. You can only claim the part of your total that exceeds the lesser of 3% of net income or a dollar ceiling set each year ($2,834 for 2025). This is why the credit usually works best claimed on the lower-income spouse's return: 3% of a smaller income is a lower bar. Expenses for both spouses and dependent children can be pooled on one return.

The window is the part everyone misses. You don't have to claim the calendar year. The CRA allows any 12-month period ending in the tax year (as long as no expense is claimed twice). A family whose big expenses ran from July 2024 to June 2025 can claim exactly that window on the 2025 return — instead of splitting the total across two years and possibly clearing the threshold in neither. Almost nobody optimizes this, because doing it by hand means re-adding receipts under every possible window.

What GRIPE actually does

GRIPE holds the complete, reimbursement-netted ledger, so the METC optimizer just… tries every window. It sweeps each possible 12-month period ending in the tax year, shows which one clears your threshold by the most, and marks claimed expenses so they can't be counted again next year. When you've picked a window, it exports the whole package: the arithmetic and the expense listing as a PDF for your records, and a CSV for your tax software.

GRIPE does arithmetic, not tax advice. The optimizer tells you which window contains the most unreimbursed money; whether and how to claim it is between you, your tax software, and — for anything unusual — a professional. Line numbers, receipts-on-request, and the travel/attendant-care corners of the METC are theirs, not ours.

Where is my data stored?

On your device and in your own private iCloud. There is no GRIPE server, no analytics, and no account. See the privacy policy — it's refreshingly short.

Why doesn't GRIPE submit claims for me?

No Canadian carrier offers a member-facing claims API, and portal scraping is fragile and against their terms. GRIPE tracks what you file and computes what to send next; the filing itself stays between you and your insurer — which is also why your data can stay private.

Which plan do we file with first?

Adults file with their own plan first and their spouse's second. For children, the industry convention is the birthday rule: the parent whose birthday falls earlier in the calendar year files first (the year of birth doesn't matter). A Health Care Spending Account goes last, so it mops up whatever the real plans leave behind. GRIPE suggests the order per person and lets you set it — custody arrangements and carrier quirks outrank conventions.

Our kid is in university — still covered?

Usually, yes: many plans cover full-time students until graduation or age 25, generally against an annual student confirmation to the carrier. GRIPE never flips a dependant to adult on a birthday for exactly this reason. When coverage genuinely ends, set the termination date on the enrolment — the claim history stays put.

A template's numbers don't match my booklet.

Templates are starting points transcribed from published booklets, and carriers amend booklets — every figure is editable under Plans, and each template shows the booklet edition it came from. Your Explanation of Benefits is always the ground truth. If you spot a genuine transcription error, tell us and we'll fix the library copy.

What's a .gripeplan file?

One plan's coverage grid — maximums, co-insurance, shared pools, referral rules — in a small file you can share. No names, no claims, no health data. Export yours from Plans and send it to a coworker on the same plan; their typing is done. Verified files for common plans live in the plan library.

Why didn't my expense cascade to the second plan?

GRIPE creates the next claim once the previous one has an answer recorded — the second insurer needs the first one's Explanation of Benefits, so the order matters. If a cascade stopped with money still on the table, the Attention list will say so.
